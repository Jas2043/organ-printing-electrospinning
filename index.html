Electrospinning Technology

Electrospinning is a technique that uses electric force to produce micro- and nanoscale fibers from polymer solutions. These fibers have high surface-to-volume ratios and tunable porosity, making them ideal for tissue engineering, drug delivery, and other biomedical applications.

The Electrospinning Process

The electrospinning process involves three key components: a high-voltage power supply, a syringe pump, and a collector. When high voltage is applied, the polymer solution forms a Taylor cone at the needle tip, and a charged jet is ejected toward the grounded collector, forming ultrafine fibers as the solvent evaporates.

Organ Printing Technology

3D bioprinting is an additive manufacturing process that deposits living cells layer-by-layer to create three-dimensional tissue structures, potentially solving the critical shortage of donor organs.

Key Components of Bioprinting

Bioinks

Specialized materials containing living cells that can be printed into complex 3D structures while maintaining cell viability and function.

Vascularization

The biggest challenge in organ printing is creating functional blood vessel networks to nourish thick tissues.

Current Challenges

  • Ensuring long-term survival and function of printed tissues
  • Recreating complex organ microarchitectures
  • Preventing immune rejection
  • Scaling up for clinical applications

My Electrospinning Research

In my experiments, I investigated electrospun nanomaterials for tissue engineering applications, particularly focusing on scaffold materials for artificial heart valves.

Experimental Methods

Using polycaprolactone (PCL) as the base material, I prepared fiber scaffolds with different morphologies by adjusting electrospinning parameters (voltage, flow rate, collection distance) and evaluated their mechanical properties and cell compatibility.

Preliminary Findings

  • Fiber alignment significantly affects scaffold mechanical strength
  • Porosity influences cell infiltration and nutrient diffusion
  • Surface modifications can enhance cell adhesion

Future Directions

Plan to combine electrospun scaffolds with bioprinting technology to develop heart valve replacements with vascular networks.
